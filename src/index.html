Making Sense of
Making Parole in
New York State

The New York State Board of Parole is the sole entity with discretionary authority to grant release to eligible individuals incarcerated with DOCCS. It is an independent body responsible for determining parole, setting parole conditions, and revoking parole when conditions have been violated.

According to DOCCS interviews with the parole board "include answering questions about prior criminal activity, custodial record, program participation, future goals and release plans."
Nowhere does it say that a certain answer or plan is the best to get you parole.
What makes someone a better candidate for parole?
Do factors such as age, race, crime, time served, facility and county of commitment play a role in predicting parole interview outcomes?
With this project, I set out to find trends in parole outcomes using only publicly available data about parole interviews between September 2021 to September 2023.

There are endless ways to explore, analyze and visualize this data.

Over these two years, the Board of Parole of New York State conducted 0 interviews with persons eligible for parole.

There were a total of 9,077 individuals interviewed.

2,760 of those individuals were interviewed more than once.

Taking a look at the outcomes
of these interviews, approximately
47% were denied parole,
34% were granted parole.

The remaining interviews were either postponed, or not yet given a decision / given a decision of "other".

For our purposes, we're going to focus on the interviews with decisions of either denied or granted.

We'll look at variables of interest such as race, age, gender, interview type and how they relate to these interview decisions.

Up til now, we've seen data to suggest a few things that, albeit interesting, aren't entirely surprising to me (given history of this country).

First, we've seen that interviews with black people are more often denied than those of any other race. And interveiws with white people are more often granted than those of any other race.

We've also seen that parole grants tend to trend upwards with age.

Let's look at something I found to be a little more surprising.

This density chart represents the estimated density of the data as related to the individual's proportion of sentence served at the time of the interview.

If we are to assume that as one serves more of their sentence (the proportion of their sentence served increases), they have a better chance of making parole, we would expect to see a normal distribution as shown with the red line.

The distribution seen here suggests that there's a sweet spot for being granted parole at around 25% of max sentence served and a spike in denials of those who've served well over half of their maximum sentence.

Here you can toggle between time served, age entered and age to see each variable's distribution for both the denied and granted groups.

The similarities between distributions of granted and denied for these variables suggests that they're not determining factors in whether someone is granted or denied parole.

Another variable we've yet to look at is the type of interview.

INITIAL: initial appearance, PIE hearings (parole immediately eligible), merit time hearings, early conditional parole for deportation hearings, medical hearings, and supplemental merit hearings.

REAPPEAR: Reappearances - release was previously denied.

REAPP: Parole violation hearings.

RECISSION: Hearings held after parole is granted but the parole board receives new information that prompts them to reconsider their decision.

SP CONSDR: Hearings held after successful appeal of parole denial.
